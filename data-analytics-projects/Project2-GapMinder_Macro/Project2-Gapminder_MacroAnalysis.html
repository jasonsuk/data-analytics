1. Introduction¶

The purpose of this analysis is to find the top business environments in Asia, and to quickly test if some factors are related to the excellence in environment.

The multiple dataset are derived from gapminder.org, and will firstly be used to assess data.

  • new business density (new registration per 1000 people, age 15 - 64)
  • ease of doing business index (1 = most business friendly regulations)
  • cost of business start-up procedure (% GNI per capita)
  • time required to start a business (days)
  • foreign direct investments inflows (net % of GDP)
  • income per person (GDP / capita, PPP$ inflation-adjusted)
  • population density per squired km
  • life expectancy in years

    • Drop the whole column due to limited information and add another relevant dataset 'Start-up procedures to register a business (number)' in relation to our topic.

  1. 'bus_regi' feature has 133 values out of 195 countries
    • non Asian countries : drop
    • Asian countries: get estimate values from the original dataset.
  1. Other NaN values: drop if non-Asian countries

# fill the missing values with compound annual growth rate (CAGR)
# function to create as below

def CAGR(df, first_col = '2018', last_col = '2028', num_periods = (2028-2018)):
    val_T = df[last_col]
    val_t = df[first_col]
    return (val_T / val_t) ** (1. / num_periods) - 1

1. Firstly, new business registration in Asia was not as active as in other countries outside Asia. The higest rank in Asia went to Singapore with 10 registration per 1000 people from 15 - 64 years' old, followed by South Korea (2.877, ranked #50) and Malaysia (2.3700, ranked #55).

In Asia Singapore follows the second, which is the higest level in Asia. South Korea also has an efficient environment for doing and starting a business, the second best in Asia, follwed by Malaysia.

Given the efficiency of starting a business (only 1 procedure to follow), New Zealand records 0.5 day to start a new busines. In Asia, Singapore take 1.5 days, the hightest level in Asia (#3 in the world) and it takes 8.0 days in South Korea (the second fatest in Asia). Although the earlier findings show that Malaysia has a relatively good business environment in Asia, but in fact, it takes 17.5 days to start a new business.

This study has limitations. For the simplicity purpose, the data was derived from a certain year position only.
